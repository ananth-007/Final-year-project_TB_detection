CLINICAL EVALUATION

  • Conduct a thorough medical history, emphasizing symptoms suggestive of TB such as persistent cough (often with sputum production) lasting more than two weeks, chest pain, fatigue, unintentional weight loss, fever (especially low-grade or intermittent), night sweats, and hemoptysis (coughing up blood).
  • Inquire about risk factors such as recent exposure to individuals with TB, travel to or residence in TB-endemic regions, immunocompromised status (e.g., HIV infection, immunosuppressive therapy), history of incarceration or homelessness, and previous TB treatment.

PHYSICAL EXAMINATION

  • Perform a comprehensive physical examination focusing on findings suggestive of TB infection:
  • Auscultate lung fields for crackles or decreased breath sounds, which may indicate pulmonary involvement.
  • Palpate lymph nodes for enlargement, particularly in the cervical and supraclavicular regions.
  • Assess for signs of extrapulmonary TB such as joint swelling, abdominal tenderness, or neurological deficits.

INITIAL DIAGNOSTIC TESTS

    Rapid Molecular Diagnostic Tests:
  • Xpert MTB/RIF Ultra and Truenat assays are nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs) that detect Mycobacterium tuberculosis DNA and mutations associated with rifampicin resistance.
  • These tests offer high sensitivity and specificity and can provide results within hours, allowing for prompt initiation of treatment.
    • Tuberculin Skin Test (TST) or Interferon-Gamma Release Assay (IGRA):
  • TST involves injecting purified protein derivative (PPD) into the skin and assessing the immune response after 48-72 hours.
  • IGRA measures interferon-gamma release by T cells in response to TB-specific antigens.
  • These tests help identify individuals with latent TB infection, although they cannot differentiate between latent infection and active disease.

FURTHER DIAGNOSTIC EVALUATION

    Chest X-ray:
  • Chest X-ray findings suggestive of TB include upper lobe infiltrates, cavitation, hilar lymphadenopathy, and pleural effusions.
  • While not diagnostic on its own, chest X-ray is valuable for evaluating the extent and severity of pulmonary involvement.
    • Sputum Smear Microscopy:
  • Acid-fast bacilli (AFB) staining of sputum samples allows for direct visualization of M. tuberculosis under a microscope.
  • While less sensitive than molecular tests, smear microscopy remains widely used in resource-limited settings.
    • Culture and Drug Susceptibility Testing (DST):
  • Culture of M. tuberculosis from sputum or other clinical specimens is the gold standard for TB diagnosis.
  • DST assesses the susceptibility of the isolated bacteria to first-line and second-line anti-TB drugs, crucial for guiding treatment decisions, especially for drug-resistant TB.

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS

    Pediatric TB Diagnosis:
  • Pediatric TB may present with nonspecific symptoms such as failure to thrive, poor appetite, and irritability.
  • Diagnostic challenges include difficulty in obtaining quality sputum samples and reliance on clinical judgment and ancillary tests.
    • HIV-Associated TB:
  • HIV infection increases the risk of TB reactivation and accelerates disease progression.
  • TB diagnosis in HIV-infected individuals may be complicated by atypical presentations and higher rates of extrapulmonary involvement.
  • HIV testing should be offered to all TB patients, and TB screening is recommended for all HIV-infected individuals, ensuring early detection and management of co-infections.

FOLLOW-UP and MONITORING

  • TB treatment typically consists of multiple antibiotics taken over several months, requiring close monitoring for treatment response and adverse effects.
  • Regular follow-up visits, including clinical assessment, sputum examination, and monitoring of liver function tests and other relevant parameters, are essential to evaluate treatment progress, ensure medication adherence, and prevent treatment failure and relapse.
