PULMONARY TB SYMPTOMS

  • Persistent Cough: The cough in pulmonary TB is often productive, meaning it produces sputum. The sputum can vary in color from white to yellow to green and may sometimes contain blood.
  • Chest Pain: Chest pain in TB can be sharp or dull and may worsen with deep breathing, coughing, or physical activity.
  • Coughing Up Blood: Hemoptysis can range from small amounts of blood streaked in sputum to larger amounts of bright red blood. It indicates damage to the lungs' blood vessels due to TB infection.

GENERAL SYMPTOMS OF TB DISEASE

  • Weakness or Fatigue: Fatigue in TB can be profound and persistent, often affecting daily activities and quality of life.
  • Weight Loss: Significant weight loss, especially in a short period, is common in TB due to the body's increased metabolic rate and decreased appetite.
  • Loss of Appetite: Anorexia in TB is often multifactorial, including factors such as cytokine release, altered taste sensation, and psychological effects.
  • Chills: Chills usually accompany fever episodes and are the body's response to an acute infection.
  • Fever: Fever in TB can be low-grade or high-grade and is often intermittent, with spikes occurring in the late afternoon or evening.
  • Night Sweats: Night sweats can be severe, soaking through clothes and bedding. They result from the body's attempt to regulate temperature during fever episodes.

FACTORS INCREASING TB RISK

  • Diabetes: Diabetes mellitus increases the risk of TB by impairing immune function and creating a favorable environment for bacterial growth.
  • Weakened Immune System: Conditions such as HIV/AIDS, cancer, organ transplantation, and immunosuppressive therapy increase susceptibility to TB by compromising the body's ability to contain the infection.
  • Malnutrition: Malnutrition, particularly deficiencies in protein and micronutrients, weakens the immune system and impairs the body's ability to mount an effective immune response against TB bacteria.
  • Tobacco Use: Smoking damages the respiratory tract, reduces ciliary function, and impairs lung immunity, making smokers more susceptible to TB infection and progression to disease.

LATENT TB INFECTION

  • People with latent TB infection have TB bacteria in their bodies but do not feel sick and cannot spread TB to others. However, they are at risk of developing active TB disease, especially if their immune system becomes compromised. Latent TB infection is diagnosed through tuberculin skin tests or interferon-gamma release assays and can be treated with preventive therapy to reduce the risk of progression to active disease.

OTHER SYMPTOMS

  • Respiratory System: TB can cause pleuritic chest pain, which worsens with deep breathing or coughing due to inflammation of the pleura. It can also lead to dyspnea (shortness of breath) and wheezing in advanced stages.
  • Systemic Symptoms: TB can present with generalized symptoms such as fatigue, fever, and weight loss, as well as specific symptoms related to the affected organs, such as abdominal pain in abdominal TB or joint pain in skeletal TB.
  • Neurological Symptoms: TB meningitis, a severe form of TB affecting the brain's membranes, presents with symptoms such as headache, neck stiffness, altered mental status, seizures, and focal neurological deficits.
  • Skin Symptoms: Cutaneous TB can manifest as nodules, ulcers, abscesses, or papules on the skin, often with a characteristic violaceous hue. It can also present as lupus vulgaris, a chronic, progressive form of TB involving the skin and underlying tissues.
