UNDERSTAND TB RISK FACTORS

  • HIV Infection: Individuals with HIV have a significantly higher risk of developing TB disease because HIV weakens the immune system, making it harder for the body to fend off TB bacteria.
  • Recent TB Infection: Those who have been recently infected with TB bacteria, especially within the last two years, are more likely to progress from latent TB infection to active TB disease.
  • Babies and Young Children: Immature immune systems in infants and young children make them more susceptible to TB disease if exposed to the bacteria.
  • People Who Inject Illegal Drugs: The act of injecting drugs can damage the lungs and weaken the immune system, increasing the risk of TB transmission and progression to disease.
  • Weakened Immune Systems: Conditions such as diabetes, certain cancers (e.g., leukemia, lymphoma), organ transplants, and prolonged use of corticosteroids or other immunosuppressive medications can compromise the immune system, making individuals more vulnerable to TB disease.
  • Elderly Individuals: Aging leads to a natural decline in immune function, making older adults more susceptible to TB infection and disease.
  • Incomplete TB Treatment in the Past: Individuals who were not properly treated for TB in the past are at higher risk of developing active TB disease. Inadequate or incomplete treatment can allow TB bacteria to persist in the body, increasing the likelihood of disease recurrence.

SCREENING & TESTING

  • TB Skin Test (TST): Also known as the Mantoux tuberculin skin test, it involves injecting a small amount of TB protein under the skin and checking for a reaction after 48 to 72 hours.
  • TB Blood Test (Interferon-Gamma Release Assay - IGRA): This blood test detects the release of interferon-gamma by white blood cells in response to TB proteins.
  • Symptom-Based Screening: Individuals experiencing symptoms such as persistent cough (lasting more than two weeks), fever, night sweats, and unexplained weight loss should seek medical attention for TB testing.
  • Contact Screening: Close contacts of individuals with active TB disease should undergo screening to detect latent TB infection.

TREATMENT FOR LATENT TB INFECTION

  • Isoniazid (INH) Therapy: This medication is commonly used to treat latent TB infection. It is usually taken daily for 6 to 9 months.
  • Rifapentine and Isoniazid (3HP): A combination of rifapentine and isoniazid, taken once weekly for 12 weeks, is an alternative treatment option for latent TB infection.
  • Directly Observed Therapy (DOT): Some healthcare providers may recommend directly observed therapy, where a healthcare worker ensures that the individual takes their medication as prescribed.

PREVENTIVE MEASURES FOR TRAVELERS

  • Avoiding Close Contact: Travelers should avoid prolonged exposure to individuals with known TB disease, especially in crowded and poorly ventilated environments.
  • Consultation with Experts: Healthcare workers traveling to areas with high TB prevalence should seek guidance from infection control or occupational health experts regarding TB prevention strategies.
  • Pre-Travel and Post-Travel Testing: Individuals anticipating prolonged exposure to TB should undergo TB testing before and after travel to detect any new infections.

GENERAL PREVENTION STRATEGIES

  • Prompt Medical Attention: Individuals experiencing symptoms suggestive of TB should seek medical attention promptly. Early diagnosis and treatment can prevent the spread of disease and improve outcomes.
  • Regular Testing: Individuals at increased risk of TB infection, such as those with HIV or close contacts of TB patients, should undergo regular testing to detect latent TB infection.
  • Completion of Treatment: It is essential for individuals prescribed treatment for latent TB infection to complete the full course of medication as directed by their healthcare provider to prevent disease progression.
  • Respiratory Hygiene: Those diagnosed with active TB disease should practice good respiratory hygiene, including covering the mouth and nose when coughing or sneezing, and promptly disposing of tissues contaminated with respiratory secretions.
  • Infection Control Measures: Healthcare facilities and institutions should implement infection control measures, including proper ventilation, use of respirators, and isolation of TB patients, to prevent transmission within healthcare settings.
